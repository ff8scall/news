## Strategic Deep-Dive

The global Printed Circuit Board (PCB) industry is undergoing a significant geographic realignment, with Thailand positioned as the primary beneficiary of the 'China Plus One' manufacturing strategy. As multinational corporations seek to mitigate geopolitical risks and enhance supply chain resilience, Thailand has successfully attracted substantial investment for high-end electronics production. The surge in demand for AI computing power—which necessitates highly complex, multi-layered PCBs with superior thermal stability—has accelerated this transition from low-cost consumer electronics toward sophisticated industrial and server-grade hardware.

However, the macro-economic shift of PCB manufacturing to Southeast Asia reveals a critical structural challenge: the persistent 'local supply gap.' While Thailand has modern assembly and fabrication facilities, the surrounding ecosystem for raw materials and specialized components remains underdeveloped. High-performance AI hardware requires advanced High-Density Interconnect (HDI) technology and specialized substrates that can handle intense heat and high-frequency signals. Currently, the chemicals, resins, and copper foils required for these processes are largely imported from East Asian hubs, creating a dependency that prevents Thailand from becoming a truly self-sufficient semiconductor and electronics hub.

For Thailand to ascend the value chain, it must foster a domestic 'Sojang' (Materials, Components, and Equipment) ecosystem that supports the entire production lifecycle.

Technically, the requirements for AI computing hardware are far more stringent than traditional computing. AI servers utilize PCBs with higher layer counts and finer line spacing to accommodate the massive bandwidth requirements of modern NPUs and GPUs. Thailand's ability to capture this lucrative market will depend on its capacity to implement advanced manufacturing processes such as mSAP (modified Semi-Additive Process) and improve its local testing and quality assurance infrastructure.

As global tech giants continue to diversify their footprints, Thailand’s industrial ambitions will be tested by its ability to bridge these internal supply gaps. The next several years will be a defining period; if Thailand can successfully integrate specialized material suppliers into its local cluster, it could transform from a secondary manufacturing site into a dominant force in the global AI hardware supply chain. Without this integration, the region risks remaining a high-end assembly point vulnerable to external supply shocks.
